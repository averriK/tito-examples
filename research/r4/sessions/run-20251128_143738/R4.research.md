## SLOT 1: Context Summary from Introduction

The South Deep Gold Mine in South Africa commissioned a site-specific Probabilistic Seismic Hazard Assessment (PSHA) conducted by the Seismology Research Centre (SRC) for the Doornpoort and South Deep Tailings Storage Facilities. The SRC assessment produced Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) values substantially higher than previously adopted design values, with 0.33 g for the 475-year return period and 1.3 g for the 10,000-year return period at the Doornpoort site, compared to prior values of 0.15 g and 0.25 g respectively. These elevated results motivated a third-party review to investigate discrepancies between SRC seismic intensity values and regional reference estimates, and to evaluate the influence of mining-related seismicity on the modeling and resultant PGA values. The specialized nature of seismic hazard assessment in the South African stable continental region setting, combined with the significant impact that higher PGA values would have on stability and deformation analyses, underscored the need for independent verification of the methodology and parameterization employed in the SRC model. ## SLOT 2: Benchmarking Analysis Summary

The SRC PSHA results exhibit systematic deviation from established reference values across multiple independent assessments. For the 475-year return period, SRC estimated 0.329 g while SRK Consulting values range from 0.055 g to 0.088 g, the Global Earthquake Model (GEM 2023) displays 0.017 g to 0.149 g, and the historical Global Seismic Hazard Assessment Program (GSHAP) estimated less than 0.01 g for the region. The SRC weighted mean exceeds the upper SRK bound by a factor of approximately 2.2. For the 10,000-year return period, SRC estimated 1.328 g compared to SRK's range of 0.232 g to 0.386 g, with SRC exceeding the upper SRK bound by a factor of approximately 3.4. These discrepancies cannot be attributed to methodological deficiencies in the SRC assessment, which follows state-of-the-art PSHA practices including well-structured logic tree frameworks, regionally appropriate ground motion prediction equations, and explicit treatment of epistemic uncertainties. The systematic elevation of hazard estimates across both return periods suggests that the increased ground motion intensities result from the specific combination of source parameters assigned to the Reitfontein Fault, which the SRC assessment treats as a discrete source with maximum magnitude $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$. The benchmarking analysis indicates that the parameterization of this fault dominates the hazard at the site, producing PGA values substantially higher than regional baselines derived from alternative source characterizations. ## SLOT 3: Reitfontein Fault Characterization Issues

The Reitfontein Fault characterization exhibits several critical deficiencies that amplify its contribution to probabilistic seismic hazard. The fault's classification as an active neotectonic structure represents a conservative engineering assumption rather than demonstrated activity, as no geological or geomorphological evidence exists for movement within the last 35,000 years, the standard threshold for active fault designation. The assigned maximum magnitude $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ exceeds the largest recorded South African earthquake (1969 Ceres event, $M_w$ 6.2) by 1.0 magnitude unit, representing a 32-fold difference in seismic moment release, and falls at the upper end of regional $M_{\text{max}}$ assignments for stable continental settings. This magnitude estimate derives from Wells and Coppersmith (1994) scaling relationships applied to the 64 km mapped fault length, but this approach provides only an upper bound on potential earthquake size without addressing the probability that such earthquakes can occur within relevant timeframes for engineering design. The fault is located 43 km from South Deep Mine, with mining operations reaching maximum depths of 4 km while seismogenic depths in stable continental regions extend to 15-25 km, indicating that Reitfontein seismicity would be primarily tectonic rather than mining-induced. The fundamental issue is that paleoseismic faults in stable continental regions typically exhibit recurrence intervals of thousands to hundreds of thousands of years, yet the PSHA parameterization implicitly assumes the fault can generate $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ events within return periods less than 10,000 years. This temporal inconsistency between the fault's likely paleoseismic character and its PSHA parameterization as an active structure drives the disproportionate contribution to short-term hazard estimates. ## SLOT 4: Corrective Measures and Recommendations

The hazard correction strategy should focus on restructuring the logic tree parameterization for the Reitfontein Fault to reflect the epistemic uncertainty associated with its paleoseismic character and temporal recurrence. The primary recommendation is to implement a multi-branch logic tree that incorporates alternative hypotheses for the fault's seismogenic potential, rather than treating the $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ scenario as a single deterministic input. The first branch should eliminate or substantially de-weight the $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ hypothesis, assigning it a low probability (0.05 to 0.15) to reflect the lack of observable neotectonic evidence and the fault's likely paleoseismic character with recurrence intervals incompatible with the 475-year and 10,000-year return periods used in design-basis calculations. Alternative branches should incorporate lower $M_{\text{max}}$ values (6.5 to 6.8) with higher weights (0.40 to 0.50 each), reflecting the uncertainty range inherent in Wells and Coppersmith (1994) scaling relationships and the regional context where the largest recorded event was $M_w$ 6.2. The second critical correction involves recalibrating the Gutenberg-Richter recurrence parameters to ensure consistency between the a-value, b-value, and assigned $M_{\text{max}}$ values within each logic tree branch. The Anderson and Luco (1983) methodology provides a physically-based approach to derive the a-value from fault slip rate constraints, ensuring that the cumulative seismic moment released by the magnitude-frequency distribution matches the long-term slip rate of the fault. This method calculates magnitude occurrence rates given known slip values using truncated exponential models, deriving the a-value from geologic slip rate while applying the regional b-value and the branch-specific $M_{\text{max}}$ attribution. For the Reitfontein Fault, applying Anderson-Luco constraints would reconcile the current epistemic inconsistency where the assigned slip rate of 1 m/Myr (itself a conservative assumption lacking direct paleoseismic confirmation) is used to justify neotectonic classification, yet the resulting recurrence parameters imply event frequencies incompatible with paleoseismic behavior. Each logic tree branch should apply Anderson-Luco constraints to ensure internal consistency between slip rate, b-value, and the branch-specific $M_{\text{max}}$, preventing the combination of conservative assumptions from compounding into unrealistic hazard estimates. The third recommendation addresses the temporal probability of full-fault rupture events. This temporal modeling can be implemented through logic tree branches that apply characteristic earthquake models with extended recurrence intervals for the largest events, while maintaining Gutenberg-Richter distributions for moderate magnitudes consistent with instrumental seismicity. The weighting structure should heavily favor branches that exclude significant contribution from $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ events to return periods less than 10,000 years, as the geological and seismotectonic evidence does not support such frequent occurrence of large-magnitude ruptures on the Reitfontein Fault. This approach maintains conservatism for very long return periods relevant to critical facilities while providing realistic hazard estimates for the design-basis return periods (475 and 10,000 years) that govern engineering decisions for the tailings storage facilities.
