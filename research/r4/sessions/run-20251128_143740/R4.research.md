## SLOT 1: Context Summary from Introduction

The South Deep Gold Mine in South Africa commissioned SLR Consulting to conduct a site-specific Probabilistic Seismic Hazard Assessment (PSHA) for the Tailings Storage Facilities at two locations: Doornpoort (New Facility) and South Deep (Four Old Dams). The seismology work, executed by the Seismology Research Centre (SRC) and documented in the June 2025 draft report, produced Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) values substantially higher than previously adopted design values from 2008-2009. The SRC PSHA yielded PGA values of 0.33 g for the 475-year return period and 1.3 g for the 10,000-year return period, representing increases from earlier design criteria of 0.15 g and 0.25 g respectively. These elevated ground motion intensities raised concerns about their impact on stability and deformation analyses, prompting SLR Consulting to recommend a third-party review focused on identifying the causes of discrepancies between SRC values and regional reference values, as well as evaluating the influence of mining-related seismicity on the resulting PGA estimates. ## SLOT 3: Reitfontein Fault Characterization Issues

The characterization of the Reitfontein Fault exhibits multiple deficiencies that significantly impact PSHA results. First, the fault lacks geological or geomorphological evidence of neotectonic activity, with no documented surface ruptures, displaced young sediments, or geomorphic markers consistent with active fault definitions requiring movement within the last 35,000 years. Singh et al. (2009) reported only minor present-day seismic activity and specifically identified the fault as requiring reassessment to determine current activity status, indicating that its designation as an active neotectonic structure represents a conservative engineering assumption rather than demonstrated activity. Second, the assigned maximum magnitude of $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ exceeds the largest recorded South African earthquake (1969 Ceres event at magnitude 6.2) by 1.0 magnitude unit, representing a 32-fold difference in seismic moment release, and falls at the extreme upper end of regional assignments where most South African PSHA models assign $M_{\text{max}}$ values ranging from 5.3 to 6.3. Third, while the 64 km rupture length provides a reasonable basis for magnitude estimation using Wells and Coppersmith (1994) scaling relationships, not all mapped fault lengths necessarily represent seismogenic lengths capable of rupturing in a single earthquake, and stable continental region earthquakes may exhibit different scaling behaviors than the active tectonic region earthquakes dominating the Wells and Coppersmith dataset. The fundamental issue is that these parameters characterize the Reitfontein Fault as capable of producing a magnitude 7.2 event within return periods relevant to engineering design (less than 10,000 years), when the fault more likely represents a paleoseismic feature with recurrence intervals of tens to hundreds of thousands of years typical of stable continental regions. ## SLOT 4: Corrective Measures and Recommendations

Based on the identified characterization deficiencies, several corrective measures can address the elevated hazard estimates through logic tree modifications for the Reitfontein Fault. First, the $M_{\text{max}} = 7.2$ branch should be assigned substantially reduced weight or eliminated from the logic tree, given the absence of neotectonic evidence, the 1.0 magnitude unit exceedance over the largest recorded South African earthquake, and the fault's apparent paleoseismic character with recurrence intervals likely exceeding 10,000 years. Logic tree approaches for epistemic uncertainty management in stable continental regions typically assign lower weights to branches representing extreme or poorly constrained scenarios, particularly when data is scarce and the proposed parameter falls at the tail of regional distributions. [KB:reitfontein.md][WEB:https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00024-022-03143-4] Second, alternative $M_{\text{max}}$ branches should be introduced reflecting more conservative estimates consistent with regional seismicity patterns, such as values in the range of 6.0 to 6.5, with higher weights assigned to these branches given their alignment with observed maximum magnitudes and typical stable continental region fault behavior. Third, the Gutenberg-Richter recurrence parameters, particularly the a-value representing the overall activity rate, must be corrected to ensure consistency with each $M_{\text{max}}$ branch. The Anderson and Luco (1983) methodology provides a physically-based approach for this correction by constraining recurrence rates through slip rate, fault area, and seismic moment budget calculations, ensuring that the magnitude-frequency distribution remains consistent with both the maximum magnitude and the fault's long-term deformation rate.
