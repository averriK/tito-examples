## SLOT 1: Tectonic regimes within 1000 km

The Louvicourt tailings storage facility site in southwestern Quebec is situated within the Canadian Shield physiographic province, which is part of the North American Craton [KB:output.md]. The Canadian Shield formed from the collision of Archean-age microcontinents during the Paleoproterozoic (2.5 to 1.6 Ga), with the microcontinents combining to form the ancient continent of Laurentia [KB:output.md]. This region is characterized as a stable continental region (SCR), specifically within the Central and Eastern North America (CENA) tectonic domain [KB:output.md]. The site is located within the Abitibi Sub-province of the Superior Province, near the boundary with the Pontiac Sub-province, marked by the Cadillac-Larder Lake fault zone [KB:output.md]. The tectonic regime controlling seismicity within the 1000 km radius is that of a cratonic interior, where seismicity is infrequent, diffuse, and generally not associated with mapped geological structures [KB:output.md] [WEB:https://www.usgs.gov/data/scr_catalog.php]. The Canadian Shield represents one of the oldest known cratons containing rocks dating back several billion years [KB:output.md]. This stable continental regime influences the PSHA by producing relatively low seismicity rates, large epicentral distances for controlling earthquakes, and ground motion attenuation characteristics distinct from those in active tectonic regions [KB:output.md]. The seismic source model implemented for this PSHA is the sixth-generation seismic hazard model of Canada (CanadaSHM6), which was specifically developed for southeastern Canada by the Geological Survey of Canada [KB:output.md]. The CanadaSHM6 incorporates areal seismic sources that represent the diffuse seismicity characteristic of stable continental regions rather than discrete fault sources [KB:output.md].

## SLOT 7: Executive summary of ground-motion record selection

The ground-motion record selection process described in chapter 8.0 of the PSHA report follows a systematic approach to develop earthquake acceleration time histories (EATHs) compatible with the site conditions and PSHA results [KB:output.md]. The selection was performed to support advanced geotechnical stability analyses of the Louvicourt TSF, with ground motions amplitude-scaled to represent the mean uniform hazard spectrum (UHS) for the 1/10,000 AEP at the site seismic ground condition of $V_{S30}$ equals 1,853 m/s [KB:output.md]. Two suites of eight, two-horizontal-component EATHs were developed for two different fundamental periods: a shorter period ($T_S$) in the range 0.25 s to 0.37 s, and a longer period ($T_L$) of approximately 1.15 s [KB:output.md]. The main selection steps were as follows. First, the 1/10,000 AEP hazard disaggregation results were reviewed, and a representative set of causal parameters (earthquake magnitude and rupture distance) was selected [KB:output.md]. Second, target secondary intensity measures (IMs) consistent with the 1/10,000 AEP hazard were estimated using an earthquake scenario based on the causal parameters from disaggregation, with magnitude $M_w$ equals 7.3 and rupture distance $r_{rup}$ equals 170 km [KB:output.md]. Third, a catalogue of candidate ground motion records with generally similar causal parameters was developed from the PEER NGA-West2 and NGA-East databases [KB:output.md]. Fourth, candidate ground motions were amplitude-scaled to be in general agreement with the target spectrum, with a focus on the fit of the horizontal geometric mean response with the target spectrum across the period ranges of interest [KB:output.md]. Fifth, the secondary IMs of each selected, scaled horizontal ground motion record were compared with the estimated conditional IM values [KB:output.md]. Sixth, the selected suite of ground motions was accepted once the geometric mean of the horizontal components agreed with the target spectrum within the period range of interest and the empirical IMs compared reasonably well with the conditional IM values [KB:output.md].

Modern literature on amplitude scaling for ground-motion records indicates that acceptable scaling factor limits vary depending on the application and analysis approach, with general recommendations typically constraining factors to relatively modest ranges [WEB:https://www.researchgate.net/publication/375888682_Selection_and_Scaling_Approaches_of_Earthquake_Time-Series_for_Structural_Engineering_Applications_A_State-of-the-Art_Review]. Recent research indicates that a reasonably limited range of amplitude scaling factors does not cause bias, though there is no clear scientific rationale for what scaling factors are acceptable and do not lead to substantial biases in structural demands [WEB:https://www.researchgate.net/publication/375888682_Selection_and_Scaling_Approaches_of_Earthquake_Time-Series_for_Structural_Engineering_Applications_A_State-of-the-Art_Review].
